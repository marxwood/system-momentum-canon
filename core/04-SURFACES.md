# Surfaces

This document defines **Surfaces** as a **canonical derivative construct**
of the System Momentum Canon.

A Surface is a **capability exposure boundary**
through which canonical systems may be perceived, interacted with,
and coordinated against.

Surfaces exist to prevent a systemic failure:

> the confusion of access with authority.

A Surface may expose powerful capabilities.
It may even allow requests for binding outcomes.

But a Surface is never itself an authority.

---

## 1. Canonical Status

Surfaces are:

- canonical
- derived
- boundary-defining
- authority-neutral

Surfaces are not implementation artifacts.
They are canonical boundary semantics that must exist
regardless of implementation.

---

## 2. What a Surface Is

A Surface is:

- an exposed boundary of capability
- a controlled point of institutional interaction
- a defined locus of admissibility constraints

Surfaces make interaction possible
without collapsing epistemic responsibility.

---

## 3. What a Surface Is Not

A Surface is not:

- an authority
- a projection
- a commitment
- a regime
- a validity mechanism
- a memory store
- a runtime

---

## 4. Surface Responsibilities

A Surface is responsible for:

- exposing what capabilities exist
- constraining capability use by admissibility rules
- making authority boundaries legible
- preventing non-authoritative layers from becoming binding by accident

A Surface is permitted to:

- request binding outcomes
- present options and choices
- expose non-binding projections

A Surface is prohibited from:

- creating authority by presentation
- laundering projections into commitments
- treating readability as truth

---

## 5. Surface vs Projection

A Projection is a **representation**.
A Surface is a **boundary of capability exposure**.

A Surface may contain projections.
A projection may be produced for use within a surface.

But:

- projections are never binding
- surfaces may expose binding capabilities
- neither projections nor surfaces are authorities

---

## 6. Surface and Binding

Surfaces may expose binding capabilities.

However:

- binding authority does not come from the surface
- binding authority comes from Core Authority rules
- binding is enacted only in the Binding/Governance domain of Mindframe

A surface may request binding.
It may not declare binding by itself.

---

## 7. Prohibitions

At the Canon and Surface level:

- technical interface descriptions are prohibited
- protocol and routing conventions are prohibited
- tool schemas are prohibited
- implementation-specific terms are prohibited

Surfaces are defined canonically only as **boundaries of capability exposure**.

---

## 8. Stability Clause

Surfaces are stable in meaning.

Changes to the canonical definition of Surfaces:

- require explicit Canon amendment
- are globally breaking by definition

---

## 9. Non-Equivalence Rules

- Surface ≠ authority
- Surface ≠ projection
- Surface ≠ commitment
- Surface ≠ regime
- Surface ≠ validity
- Surface ≠ runtime
- Surface ≠ implementation
