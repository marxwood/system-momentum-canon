# Regimes

A **regime** is a canonical constraint framework that defines how claims may be accepted, evaluated, contested, and invalidated under different conditions.

Regimes exist because a single global standard of validity is insufficient across all contexts, domains, and operational modes.

Regimes do not redefine meaning (KSA).  
Regimes do not execute actions (Derivatives).  
Regimes do not prescribe lived practice (Doctrines).

---

## 1. Purpose

Regimes provide structured variability without semantic drift.

They:

- define what kinds of claims are admissible
- constrain what counts as acceptable evidence
- define what types of contradictions matter
- specify what “validity” means operationally (without turning it into truth)

---

## 2. Regime Components

A regime is defined by:

- **Claim classes**: what types of claims are permitted (e.g., observation, report, inference)
- **Evidence classes**: what types of evidence may support a claim
- **Evaluation rules**: how support and contradiction are handled
- **Invalidation rules**: what causes a claim to lose validity
- **Escalation rules**: when the regime requires higher scrutiny or different handling

---

## 3. Default Regime Set

The Canon supports a minimal default set of regimes.

These regimes represent a spectrum from low-constraint to high-constraint validity handling.

1. **Open Regime**  
   Admits broad claim types, low barriers for inclusion, high tolerance for uncertainty.  
   Use when exploration matters more than stability.

2. **Disciplined Regime**  
   Admits claims with explicit provenance and minimal evidence requirements.  
   Default for systems that must remain coherent.

3. **Adversarial Regime**  
   Assumes active contradiction pressure; emphasizes dispute handling and contradiction tracking.  
   Use where contested claims are expected.

4. **High-Assurance Regime**  
   Strict admissibility; requires strong evidence classes and explicit evaluation trails.  
   Use when downstream decisions are high-stakes.

5. **Locked Regime**  
   Claims are admitted only under tightly controlled procedures; semantic and validity changes require explicit governance.  
   Use for canonical baselines and long-lived public artifacts.

---

## 4. Regime Switching

A claim may be evaluated under different regimes, but:

- Regime switching must be explicit.
- A claim retains provenance of the regime(s) applied.
- A “valid under regime X” statement does not imply validity under other regimes.

---

## 5. Relationship to Projections

Projections may display claims from multiple regimes.

They must not:

- collapse regimes into a single score
- hide regime context
- present regime-specific validity as universal truth

---

## 6. Minimal Regime Invariants

- A regime must declare admissible claim classes.
- A regime must declare admissible evidence classes.
- A regime must declare invalidation triggers.
- Regime context must travel with claims.
