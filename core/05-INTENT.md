# Intent

This document defines **Intent** as the canonical concept
through which **institutional agency** is declared
and **institutional posture** is selected,
constraining epistemic admissibility.

Intent exists to prevent a common systemic failure:

> institutional acts occurring without an explicit declaration
> of what kind of epistemic responsibility is being assumed.

Intent is a **canonical precondition** for any legitimate institutional act.

Intent is not a workflow.
Intent is not a flow.
Intent is not execution.

Intent is a **meaning-based constraint** on what kinds of epistemic acts
may be treated as institutionally valid.

---

## 1. Canonical Status

Intent is part of the **Core Canon** and exists at a **foundational semantic level**.

It is:

- mandatory for all institutional acts
- semantically closed
- non-negotiable
- auditable
- stable by default

Intent is not a domain, object, or projection.
Intent is the declaration of **institutional epistemic posture**.

---

## 2. What Intent Is

Intent is an explicit institutional declaration of agency and posture that:

- constrains what kinds of epistemic acts are admissible,
- constrains which domains may produce binding outputs,
- constrains how outcomes may be interpreted,
- prevents authority leakage across Mindframe domains.

Intent precedes any institutional act.

Without declared Intent, no act may be treated as institutional.

---

## 3. What Intent Is Not

Intent is not:

- an event
- a user action
- a heuristic
- an automatism
- a system inference
- a task or workflow
- a routing mechanism

Intent does not execute anything.
Intent does not define ordering.
Intent does not define flows.

---

## 4. Relationship to Mindframe

Mindframe defines epistemic domains as jurisdictions.

Intent does not sequence domains.
Intent constrains **admissibility** of acts and outcomes across domains.

In particular, Intent:

- permits certain kinds of outcomes,
- prohibits certain kinds of authority claims,
- determines whether institutional memory may be written,
- determines whether binding may occur.

---

## 5. Relationship to Governance

Intent and Governance are distinct but bound:

- **Intent (semantics)**: what kinds of institutional posture exist
- **Governance (authority)**: who may activate which Intent, when, and under what conditions

Intent is defined at Canon level.
Governance operationalizes Intent within institutional constraints.

Intent without Governance is inert.
Governance without Intent has nothing legitimate to activate.

---

## 6. Canonical Intent Taxonomy

The Canon recognizes the following **closed set of Intents**.

Each Intent defines:

- the epistemic purpose of the act,
- which Mindframe domains may produce outcomes,
- which kinds of outcomes are prohibited,
- and whether institutional obligation may be created.

---

### I1. Exploratory

**Purpose:** thinking, exploration, hypothesis generation.

**Permitted:**

- Perceptual intake
- Cognitive reasoning
- Interpretive projection generation

**Prohibited:**

- institutional commitment (IEM writes)
- binding / governance authorization
- validity declarations treated as institutional truth

**Permitted outcomes:**

- hypotheses
- candidate interpretations
- non-binding projections

---

### I2. Semantic Inquiry

**Purpose:** understanding meaning, classification, and definition.

**Permitted:**

- Cognitive reasoning
- KSA read access (meaning inspection)
- Interpretive projections (explanatory)

**Prohibited:**

- IEM writes
- binding
- institutional truth assertions about the world

**Permitted outcomes:**

- clarifications
- candidate classifications
- non-binding semantic explanations

---

### I3. Archival

**Purpose:** preserving input without conclusion or institutional commitment.

**Permitted:**

- Perceptual intake
- provenance attachment
- IEM recording as _uncommitted archival material_

**Prohibited:**

- binding
- meaning changes (KSA modifications)
- truth claims derived from reasoning

**Permitted outcomes:**

- preserved evidence
- preserved input
- provenance records
- later-evaluable material

---

### I4. Epistemic Commitment

**Purpose:** institutional assumption of responsibility for a claim.

**Permitted:**

- Perceptual intake
- Cognitive reasoning
- KSA reference (meaning must be stable)
- Governance binding
- IEM commitment recording
- Validity and regime evaluation as required by Authority

**Required:**

- named authority
- provenance
- validity conditions
- regime admissibility

**Prohibited:**

- silent commitment
- commitment without declared authority
- laundering projections into commitments

**Permitted outcomes:**

- institutional commitments
- recorded claims with responsibility
- binding obligations (as defined by Authority)

---

### I5. Reflection

**Purpose:** learning from institutional behavior over time.

**Permitted:**

- IEM reading
- reflective critique
- interpretive projections (comparative / diagnostic)

**Prohibited:**

- direct modification of IEM commitments
- binding decisions enacted by reflection alone
- meaning redefinition by critique

**Permitted outcomes:**

- assessments
- critiques
- amendment proposals (non-binding)
- detected inconsistencies and risks

---

## 7. Intent Rules

The following rules are non-negotiable:

1. **Intent precedes institutional acts**  
   If there is no declared Intent, the act is non-institutional.

2. **Intent constrains admissibility**  
   Domains may operate, but outcomes are illegitimate if prohibited by Intent.

3. **Intent does not define order**  
   Intent is jurisdictional, not procedural.

4. **Binding requires an Intent that permits binding**  
   Only Epistemic Commitment (I4) permits binding and IEM commitments.

5. **Intent is auditable**  
   Every institutional act must have a recorded Intent.

6. **Intent cannot be silently inferred as binding**  
   Systems may propose candidate Intent, but binding requires explicit declaration.

---

## 8. Closure Rule

The set of canonical Intents is **closed**.

No Intent may be added, removed, or redefined
without an explicit Canon amendment.

Any institutional act activated by an undeclared
or non-canonical Intent is non-canonical by definition.

---

## 9. Non-Equivalence Rules

- Intent ≠ event
- Intent ≠ task
- Intent ≠ workflow
- Intent ≠ projection purpose
- Intent ≠ user action
- Intent ≠ system trigger
- Intent ≠ automation rule

Intent is a canonical declaration of **institutional posture**, nothing else.

---

## 10. Prohibition of Implementation Concerns

At the Canon level:

- intent routing mechanisms are prohibited
- intent detection algorithms are prohibited
- automatic intent inference is prohibited
- intent-to-workflow mappings are prohibited
- tool schemas are prohibited

Any reference to technical implementation of Intent
at this level constitutes a canonical violation.

Implementation-level concerns belong exclusively to operational specifications.

---

## 11. Stability Clause

Intent is stable by default.

Changes to Intent:

- require explicit Canon amendment
- are versioned at Canon level
- are globally breaking by definition

Silent evolution of Intent is forbidden.
