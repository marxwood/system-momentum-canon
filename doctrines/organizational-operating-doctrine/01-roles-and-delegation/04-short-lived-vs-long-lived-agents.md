# Short-Lived vs Long-Lived Execution Units

Organizations use both:

- short-lived execution
- and long-lived execution

Short-lived execution is session work.

Long-lived execution is continuity work.

Both are necessary.

Both are dangerous if unmanaged.

---

## Why lifecycle matters

Lifecycle matters because persistence creates power.

A long-lived execution unit accumulates:

- context
- permissions
- relationships
- and credibility

Accumulated credibility becomes authority.

Authority becomes immunity.

Immunity becomes capture.

Short-lived execution has the opposite risk.

It produces:

- speed
- and deniability

Deniability produces blame diffusion.

---

## Short-lived execution

Short-lived execution is suited for:

- bounded tasks
- reversible actions
- narrow scopes
- and high-frequency work

Its main failure mode is lack of ownership.

---

## Long-lived execution

Long-lived execution is suited for:

- institutional memory
- ongoing monitoring
- governance functions
- and continuity under change

Its main failure mode is unbounded growth of power.

---

## The organizational hazard

The hazard is lifecycle drift.

Lifecycle drift occurs when:

- short-lived work becomes long-lived without oversight
- long-lived work accumulates permissions without review
- and ownership becomes unclear

Lifecycle drift produces silent capture.

---

## Failure modes

### Persistent permission creep

Long-lived units accumulate privileges.

This becomes unbounded capability.

### Continuity monopoly

One unit becomes the only carrier of knowledge.

This creates single-point capture.

### Disposable execution

Short-lived units perform actions without repair ownership.

This produces permanent drift.

### Institutional amnesia

Everything is short-lived.

The organization forgets.

Forgetting makes capture cheap.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable organization must preserve:

1. explicit lifecycle classification for execution units
2. periodic review of long-lived permissions and scope
3. ownership bindings for both session work and continuity work
4. redundancy of institutional memory carriers

If lifecycle is unmanaged, persistence becomes capture.
