# Cross-Cultural Translation

Cross-cultural translation is not language.

It is legitimacy transfer.

Mechanisms that feel neutral in one culture can feel like humiliation in another.

Humiliation is a violence threshold crossing.

This is why translation errors often become conflict accelerators.

---

## Translation is not universalism

Universalism is the belief that one mechanism fits all.

Universalism fails because:

- cultures have different sacred constraints
- cultures have different humiliation triggers
- cultures have different legitimacy interfaces

Ignoring these differences does not produce rational order.

It produces backlash.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is translation being done through:

- moral superiority
- civilizational ranking
- and “education” framing

These are humiliation vectors.

Humiliation produces identity hardening.

Identity hardening makes evidence stop traveling.

When evidence stops traveling, coordination collapses.

---

## Failure modes

### Mechanism humiliation

A mechanism is experienced as degradation.

This produces rejection regardless of its function.

### Sacred value collision

A mechanism violates sacred constraints.

Sacred constraints are not negotiable through arguments.

### Translation as propaganda

Mechanisms are exported with narratives.

Narratives trigger immune response.

### Translation as erasure

Local meaning systems are dismissed as primitive.

This produces resentment and retaliation readiness.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable cross-cultural order must preserve:

1. mechanism-first translation
2. explicit humility about sacred constraints
3. dignity preservation in framing
4. adaptation rather than replacement

If translation becomes humiliation, global coordination becomes conflict.
