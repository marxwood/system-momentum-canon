# Trust as Infrastructure

Trust is not a feeling.

Trust is infrastructure.

It is the condition under which:

- agreements can be made
- institutions can function
- markets can coordinate
- and reform can occur without collapse panic

A society without trust can still have order.

But it will tend toward coercion.

Because coercion replaces trust.

---

## Trust is not moral goodness

Trust does not require virtue.

Trust requires:

- predictability
- accountability
- and repair

When accountability collapses, trust collapses.

When trust collapses, society becomes capturable.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is trust collapse being treated as:

- a cultural defect
- a moral failure
- or an ideological disagreement

Trust collapse is usually structural.

It is produced by:

- selective enforcement
- narrative immunity
- monopoly and lock-in
- dependence weaponization
- accountability theater

When these exist, trust cannot survive.

---

## Trust and correction capacity

Trust is required for correction.

Without trust:

- contradiction becomes hostility
- belief update becomes betrayal
- negotiation becomes naive
- reform becomes panic trigger

This is why trust collapse often precedes authoritarian stabilization.

Authoritarian stabilization is a trust substitute.

It produces order.

It does not produce correction.

---

## Failure modes

### Trust privatization

Trust exists only inside tribes.

Society fragments into incompatible worlds.

Fragmentation produces longing for closure.

Closure is supplied by coercion.

### Trust as authority worship

Trust is outsourced to authority.

Authority becomes immune.

Immune authority becomes power.

### Trust as cynicism

Trust is replaced by cynicism.

Cynicism produces apathy.

Apathy produces capture.

### Trust as fear compliance

Trust is replaced by obedience.

Obedience stabilizes.

It also destroys correction capacity.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. accountability ledgers that are inspectable
2. repair mechanisms that are real
3. exit paths that make coercion expensive

If trust collapses, coercion becomes the stabilizer.
