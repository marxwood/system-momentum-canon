# Monopoly and Lock-In

Monopoly is not only an economic condition.

Monopoly is a political condition.

Monopoly is the ability to govern through lack of alternatives.

Lock-in is monopoly made durable.

Together, they convert economics into coercion.

---

## Monopoly is power

Monopoly power is the ability to:

- set terms unilaterally
- remove exit paths
- punish through deprivation
- and make dependence permanent

A monopolized society does not need overt violence.

Compliance becomes the rational choice.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is not that large actors exist.

The hazard is monopoly plus lock-in.

When lock-in exists:

- reform becomes impossible
- accountability becomes symbolic
- and correction becomes too costly

This is capture through economics.

---

## Lock-in mechanics

Lock-in is produced through:

- switching costs
- dependence cascades
- regulatory capture
- network effects
- infrastructure control
- and identity-coded consumption

Lock-in is rarely framed as coercion.

It is framed as efficiency.

Efficiency can be real.

It can also be the most stable form of domination.

---

## Failure modes

### Monopoly as inevitability

Society believes monopoly is natural.

This produces resignation.

Resignation produces apathy.

Apathy produces capture.

### Monopoly as moral hero

Monopoly is treated as savior.

This produces narrative immunity.

Narrative immunity accelerates capture.

### Monopoly as invisible infrastructure

Monopoly becomes background.

People stop noticing.

This is the most stable form.

### Monopoly enforced by dependence

Exit is punished.

Contradiction becomes suicidal.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. real exit paths
2. constraints on lock-in mechanisms
3. redundancy in critical infrastructure

If monopoly becomes durable, coercion becomes economically cheap.
