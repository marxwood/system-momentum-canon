# Exit Paths

Exit is not escape fantasy.

Exit is the structural alternative to coercion.

If people cannot exit, they can be governed through dependence.

If people can exit, coercion becomes expensive.

This is why exit paths are one of the deepest civilizational stabilizers.

---

## Exit is a power limiter

Exit limits power because it changes the threat model.

When exit exists:

- punishment loses effectiveness
- monopoly loses durability
- dependence becomes negotiable
- and legitimacy must compete

When exit does not exist:

- obedience becomes rational
- contradiction becomes suicidal
- capture becomes stable

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is exit paths being removed while freedom remains symbolic.

A society can have:

- formal rights
- and no exit

Formal rights without exit are fragile.

They do not survive pressure.

---

## Exit is multi-layered

Exit is not only geographic.

Exit layers include:

- employment mobility
- housing mobility
- market access mobility
- educational mobility
- legal safety mobility
- institutional mobility
- information mobility

The more layers exist, the harder it is to capture society.

---

## Failure modes

### Exit criminalization

Exit is treated as betrayal.

This produces:

- loyalty capture
- identity coercion
- and permanent dependence

### Exit cost inflation

Exit is possible, but financially impossible.

This produces coercion without visible violence.

### Exit through humiliation

Exit requires public submission.

Humiliation is a violence threshold crossing.

### Exit collapse under crisis

Exit paths disappear during crisis.

Crisis becomes capture accelerator.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. at least one survivable exit path per dependence domain
2. protection for those who exit
3. redundancy of exit mechanisms

If exit disappears, coercion becomes economically cheap.
