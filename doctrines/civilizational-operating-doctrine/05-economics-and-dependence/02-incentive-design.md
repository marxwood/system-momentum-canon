# Incentive Design

Civilizations are not governed primarily by laws.

They are governed by incentives.

Incentives determine what becomes:

- rewarded
- punished
- tolerated
- and normalized

A society can declare any values it wants.

Incentives decide what actually happens.

---

## Incentives are not morality

Incentives are not ethics.

Incentives are the operational layer where behavior becomes rational.

If a behavior is rewarded, it will spread.

If a behavior is punished, it will vanish.

This is true even when the behavior is:

- destructive
- corrupt
- or epistemically toxic

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is incentives that reward:

- capture
- dependence creation
- epistemic pollution
- humiliation
- and emergency escalation

When incentives reward these, the society will drift into coercion without needing ideology.

---

## Incentive gaming is inevitable

Incentives will be gamed.

This is not a failure of character.

It is a structural property.

A correction-capable order must assume gaming.

It must design for:

- detectability
- bounded damage
- and reversibility

If gaming is not assumed, capture will occur.

---

## Incentives and legitimacy

Incentives shape legitimacy by making certain actions feel:

- necessary
- profitable
- and normal

This is how societies drift into:

- monopoly
- corruption networks
- and coercive enforcement

while maintaining the appearance of legality.

---

## Failure modes

### Perverse incentives

The system rewards what it claims to oppose.

This produces cynicism.

Cynicism produces apathy.

Apathy produces capture.

### Incentive opacity

People cannot see what is rewarded.

This makes correction impossible.

### Incentive centralization

A small number of actors control rewards.

This produces dependence capture.

### Incentive acceleration

Speed and scale rewards override consequence mapping.

This produces systemic blowback.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. visibility of reward structures
2. ability to punish gaming
3. reversibility of incentive-driven damage

If incentives cannot be inspected, the society is governed by invisible power.
