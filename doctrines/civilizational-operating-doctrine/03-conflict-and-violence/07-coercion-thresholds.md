# Coercion Thresholds

Coercion is not only violence.

Coercion is the condition where compliance is produced primarily through fear.

Fear can be produced without physical harm.

Fear can be produced through:

- deprivation
- humiliation
- dependence
- selective enforcement
- unpredictability of punishment

This module defines coercion as a threshold phenomenon.

---

## Coercion stabilizes order

Coercion works.

This is why it is dangerous.

Coercion produces:

- predictability
- obedience
- and fast coordination

It also produces:

- silence
- cynicism
- and loss of correction capacity

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is coercion being normalized as “necessary”.

Once coercion is normalized, it becomes self-reinforcing.

Because coercion destroys the mechanisms that could replace it.

This is how societies become trapped.

---

## Threshold crossings into coercion

This doctrine does not list regimes.

It lists threshold crossings.

### 1) Punishment becomes asymmetric

Friends are protected.
Enemies are punished.

### 2) Punishment becomes unpredictable

Actors cannot know what will trigger punishment.

Unpredictability produces fear.

### 3) Exit paths disappear

Dependence becomes unescapable.

### 4) Contradiction becomes punishable

Truth becomes dangerous.

### 5) Accountability becomes humiliation

Repair becomes impossible.

Each crossing reduces correction capacity.

---

## Failure modes

### Coercion as stability addiction

Society becomes dependent on coercion to coordinate.

Coordination without fear becomes unimaginable.

### Coercion as moral purification

Coercion is framed as justice.

Justice framing makes coercion permanent.

### Coercion as deterrence escalation

Each side escalates coercion to deter the other.

This produces retaliation loops.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. symmetry of enforcement
2. predictability of punishment
3. survivable exits
4. safe contradiction

If these fail, coercion becomes the stabilizer.
