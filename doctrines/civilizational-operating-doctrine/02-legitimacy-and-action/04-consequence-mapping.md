# Consequence Mapping

Civilizations rarely fail because they choose evil.

They fail because they choose actions without mapping consequences.

At scale, consequences are not linear.

They are delayed.

They are distributed.

They are amplified through institutions and incentives.

This is why “good intent” is not sufficient.

---

## Consequence mapping is not prediction

Consequence mapping is not forecasting.

It is not certainty.

It is the discipline of asking:

- what this action enables
- what it normalizes
- what it incentivizes
- what it makes inevitable later
- what it destroys that cannot be rebuilt

A society that does not ask these questions becomes a society that can only react.

Reaction is how coercion becomes normal.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is that consequence mapping is replaced by:

- slogans
- moral panic
- enemy labeling
- “we had no choice”
- emergency logic

These substitutes feel decisive.

They are not corrective.

They are accelerants.

---

## Second-order consequences are the main consequences

At civilizational scale, the main consequences are second-order.

The action itself may be small.

The precedent is large.

The system learns.

Institutions adapt.

Incentives reconfigure.

What becomes normal becomes permanent.

This is how societies drift into coercion without noticing.

---

## The doctrine rule

The doctrine rule is:

**Treat precedent as consequence.**

Not as theory.

As operational reality.

If an action becomes precedent, it becomes permission.

Permission becomes future action.

Future action becomes order.

---

## Failure modes

### Single-step thinking

Only immediate effects are considered.

This produces:

- policy whiplash
- endless crisis management
- escalating coercion

### Moral licensing

Because intent is framed as good, consequences are ignored.

This produces unbounded action.

### Enemy fixation

Consequences are mapped only for enemies.

Friends receive immunity.

This produces capture.

### Panic mapping

Consequences are replaced by fear scenarios.

Fear scenarios justify closure.

Closure destroys correction.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve three mapping questions:

1. What does this action normalize?
2. What does this action incentivize?
3. What does this action destroy that cannot be repaired?

If these questions disappear, legitimacy becomes a license.

And licenses become coercion.
