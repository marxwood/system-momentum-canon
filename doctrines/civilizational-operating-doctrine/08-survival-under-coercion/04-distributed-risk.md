# Distributed Risk

Coercive orders prefer centralized resistance.

Centralization makes suppression cheap.

Distributed risk is the opposite.

It is the discipline of making correction capacity survivable by ensuring that:

- no single person is essential
- no single group is the sole carrier
- no single channel is the only path
- no single artifact is the only evidence

Distributed risk is not paranoia.

It is continuity engineering.

---

## Why distribution matters

Under coercion, the main civilizational failure mode is:

**single-point martyrdom.**

Martyrdom can inspire.

It also ends the carrier.

Coercive orders often allow martyrdom.

Because martyrdom collapses continuity.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is resistance or correction capacity being organized as:

- a single leader
- a single organization
- a single platform
- a single archive
- a single narrative

These structures are emotionally satisfying.

They are operationally fragile.

Fragility invites capture.

---

## Failure modes

### Hero dependence

The system depends on heroic individuals.

Heroism is not scalable.

### Institutional dependence

Correction depends on one institution.

Institutions are capturable.

### Channel dependence

Communication depends on one platform.

Platforms are controllable.

### Archive dependence

Evidence depends on one store.

Stores can be erased.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable society under coercion must preserve:

1. multiple independent carriers of evidence
2. multiple independent coordination paths
3. contribution models that do not require exposure
4. continuity plans that assume losses

If risk is not distributed, coercion becomes permanent.
