# Collective Action and Free Riders

Civilizations are not coordinated by good intentions.

They are coordinated by equilibria.

The most basic equilibrium problem is free riding.

Free riding is not a moral defect.

It is a structural outcome when:

- contribution is costly
- benefits are shared
- punishment is risky
- and trust is low

This module treats free riding as an inevitability.

Not as a shame category.

---

## Why free riding matters

When free riding dominates:

- institutions decay
- public goods collapse
- trust collapses
- cynicism spreads
- and coercion becomes attractive

Coercion solves free riding.

It also destroys correction capacity.

This is why free riding is a capture gateway.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is free riding being processed through:

- humiliation
- scapegoating
- and purity tests

These responses create enemies.

Enemies create retaliation loops.

Retaliation loops produce coercive closure.

---

## What makes contribution possible

Collective contribution becomes stable when:

- contribution can be distributed
- risk can be reduced
- punishment can be bounded
- and accountability is inspectable

This is not moral inspiration.

This is infrastructure.

---

## Failure modes

### Moralization

Free riders are treated as evil.

This produces humiliation and backlash.

### Naive trust

The system assumes contribution will happen voluntarily.

This produces decay.

### Punishment escalation

Punishment becomes harsh.

Harsh punishment produces fear.

Fear produces silence.

Silence produces capture.

### Elite exemption

Elites are exempt from contribution.

This destroys legitimacy.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. contribution models that reduce exposure
2. bounded punishment gradients (not annihilation)
3. accountability ledgers for both contributors and enforcers

If free riding cannot be managed without humiliation, coercion becomes the stabilizer.
