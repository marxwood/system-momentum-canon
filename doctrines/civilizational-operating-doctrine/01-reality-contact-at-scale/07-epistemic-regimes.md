# Epistemic Regimes

A civilization does not have a single epistemology.

It has epistemic regimes.

An epistemic regime is a stable pattern of:

- what counts as evidence
- who is allowed to speak
- who is trusted
- how contradiction is handled
- how belief updates occur
- what risks exist for dissent

Epistemic regimes are not theories.

They are operating conditions.

---

## Why epistemic regimes matter

Civilizational coordination depends less on “truth” than on:

- which epistemic regime dominates
- how regimes compete
- whether contradiction is survivable

A society can have strong truth pockets and still collapse if:

- the dominant epistemic regime is closed
- or if regimes are locked into identity war

---

## The core regimes

This doctrine does not classify regimes by ideology.

It classifies them by operational properties.

### 1) Correction-capable regime

- evidence primitives exist
- provenance is valued
- contradiction is protected
- uncertainty is tolerated
- belief update is survivable

### 2) Authority regime

- evidence is secondary
- authority decides what is real
- contradiction is disloyalty
- belief update is obedience

### 3) Tribal regime

- evidence is identity-coded
- contradiction is humiliation
- belief update is betrayal
- credibility is inherited through belonging

### 4) Performance regime

- evidence is aesthetic
- credibility is theater
- contradiction is content
- belief update is attention-driven

### 5) Apathy regime

- evidence is irrelevant
- contradiction is pointless
- belief update is absent
- coordination is minimal and fear-based

These regimes can coexist.

The danger is dominance without correction.

---

## Regime transitions

Societies rarely jump directly from correction-capable to collapse.

They transition through intermediate states:

- correction becomes expensive
- contradiction becomes socially dangerous
- uncertainty becomes intolerable
- credibility becomes theater
- authority becomes attractive

This is how capture becomes normal.

---

## Failure modes

### Regime war

Multiple epistemic regimes compete.

The competition becomes identity war.

This produces:

- humiliation
- censorship
- moral intoxication
- purges

Correction becomes impossible.

### Regime monopoly

A single regime dominates and closes.

This produces:

- stability
- obedience
- silence
- slow decay

### Regime fragmentation

No regime dominates.

This produces:

- chaos
- exhaustion
- longing for closure

Closure is supplied by coercion.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. a space where correction-capable epistemics can operate
2. the ability to contest authority without annihilation
3. the ability to prevent tribal epistemics from becoming the sole coordination mechanism

This is not a moral preference.

It is a requirement for non-coercive coordination.
