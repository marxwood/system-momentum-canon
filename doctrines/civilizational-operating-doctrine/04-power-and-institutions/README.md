# Power and Institutions

At civilizational scale, institutions are not ideals.

They are machines.

They are built to coordinate action under constraints.

They produce:

- permissions
- punishments
- incentives
- and stability

They also produce capture.

This section treats institutions as operational machinery.

Not as sacred objects.
Not as moral symbols.
Not as identity.

---

## Power is not a theory

Power is not a metaphor.

Power is the ability to:

- impose costs
- deny resources
- grant permissions
- remove protections
- and shape what can be safely said

Most civilizational analysis fails because it treats power as:

- illegitimate by definition
- or legitimate by definition

This doctrine treats power as an attractor.

If you do not map it, it maps you.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is not that institutions fail.

Institutions always fail.

The hazard is that failure becomes:

- permanent
- normalized
- and immune to correction

This is capture.

Capture is not a single event.

It is a stable operating state.

---

## What this section covers

This section defines:

- where power lives
- how institutions function as machines
- how constraints erode
- how capture happens
- how reform can occur without collapse
- how accountability can exist without purge
- how elites coordinate
- what constraints survive pressure

This is not a design for perfect institutions.

It is a doctrine for correction-capable order.

---

## The stance

The stance of this section is:

**Institutions must preserve correction capacity.**

Not through moral purity.

Through:

- constraints that survive pressure
- accountability that does not require annihilation
- redundancy that prevents monopoly
- and reform that does not trigger chaos panic

---

## Failure modes this section defends against

This section exists because institutional failure is predictable:

- institutions become identity symbols
- institutions become tools for factional punishment
- constraints are suspended under emergency logic
- accountability becomes purge
- elites coordinate through dependence networks
- corruption becomes a stabilizer
- fear becomes governance style

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. power mapping (knowing where punishment lives)
2. constraints that survive pressure
3. accountability without purge
4. reform without collapse panic

Without these, institutions become machines of coercion.

And coercion becomes the stabilizer.
