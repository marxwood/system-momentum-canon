# Power Maps

Civilizations do not collapse because they lack ideals.

They collapse because they misread power.

Power is not what people claim.

Power is what can punish.

If you do not map power, you will be governed by it blindly.

---

## What a power map is

A power map is not a theory of society.

It is an operational model that answers:

- who can punish whom
- who can deny resources
- who can grant permissions
- who can remove protections
- who can rewrite consequences
- who can silence contradiction

Power maps are not moral.

They are descriptive.

---

## Why power maps matter

Without power maps, societies become vulnerable to:

- symbolic institutions
- legitimacy theater
- narrative capture
- and invisible coercion

People argue about what should be true.

Meanwhile, power moves.

---

## Power is multi-layered

At scale, power is layered.

It exists in:

- formal institutions
- enforcement organs
- economic dependence networks
- media credibility systems
- social punishment systems
- access to resources and exit paths

Formal authority is only one layer.

A society that maps only formal authority is blind.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is that power becomes invisible.

When power is invisible:

- accountability cannot be assigned
- reform becomes naive
- contradiction becomes suicidal
- and capture becomes permanent

Invisible power is the most stable form of power.

---

## Failure modes

### Formalism collapse

Society believes that written structures define power.

In reality, punishment defines power.

### Moralism collapse

Society believes that “good intent” defines power.

In reality, capacity defines power.

### Narrative collapse

Society believes that controlling language controls power.

In reality, controlling consequences controls power.

### Cynicism collapse

Society believes power is absolute and unchangeable.

This produces apathy reality.

Apathy reality is stable.

It is also capturable.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve one discipline:

**Always ask: who can punish whom?**

If this question cannot be asked safely, the order is already coercive.
