# Censorship and Self-Censorship

Censorship is not only removal of speech.

Censorship is the control of what can be safely said.

Self-censorship is the internalization of that control.

At civilizational scale, self-censorship is more important than censorship.

Because self-censorship is cheaper.

And it is stable.

---

## Why censorship emerges

Censorship emerges when power decides that correction is dangerous.

Not necessarily because truth is hated.

Because contradiction threatens:

- legitimacy claims
- elite coordination
- and fear-based order

A captured order does not primarily seek silence.

It seeks predictability.

Predictability is produced by fear.

---

## The civilizational hazard

The hazard is not that some speech is constrained.

Every society constrains speech.

The hazard is when censorship targets:

- evidence primitives
- provenance
- contradiction
- and uncertainty

When censorship targets these, correction becomes impossible.

---

## Self-censorship as equilibrium

Self-censorship is an equilibrium where actors learn:

- what is safe
- what is dangerous
- what is career-ending
- what is socially annihilating

They adjust without being forced.

This produces stability.

It destroys correction.

---

## Failure modes

### Chilling effect

Actors stop asking questions.

They stop contradicting.

They stop inspecting.

The system becomes uncorrectable.

### Selective censorship

Censorship applies only to enemies.

Friends receive narrative immunity.

This produces capture.

### Censorship through ambiguity

Rules are vague.

Enforcement is unpredictable.

Unpredictability produces fear.

Fear produces obedience.

### Moralized censorship

Censorship is framed as virtue.

Virtue framing makes censorship permanent.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable order must preserve:

1. safe contradiction
2. safe uncertainty
3. safe inspection of evidence and provenance

If these become punishable, the order is already coercive.
