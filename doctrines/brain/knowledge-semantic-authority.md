# Knowledge / Semantic Authority Domain — Doctrinal View

This document explains the Knowledge / Semantic Authority Domain (KSA)
from a human and systemic perspective.

It clarifies what semantic authority is, why it exists,
and why confusing it with memory or reasoning is one of the most
destructive errors a system can make.

---

## 1. What Semantic Authority Is

Semantic Authority is the domain where **meaning is fixed**.

It answers questions such as:

- What does this term mean?
- What is the boundary of a concept?
- What distinctions are valid?

KSA does not observe the world.
It does not reason about possibilities.
It defines **what words and structures mean**.

Meaning is not discovered here.
It is **declared and maintained**.

---

## 2. Knowledge vs Meaning

Knowledge and meaning are often confused.

- **Meaning** defines _what something is_
- **Knowledge** concerns _what is known about occurrences_

KSA governs meaning.
It does not govern facts, events, or claims.

A system with weak semantic authority
cannot reason consistently,
no matter how much data it has.

---

## 3. Ontology vs Living Semantics

KSA is not a static ontology.

It is a **living semantic system** that:

- may evolve through governance
- may clarify or refine definitions
- must remain internally consistent

However, evolution of meaning:

- is explicit
- is slow
- is auditable

Silent semantic drift is forbidden.

---

## 4. What Never Belongs Here

KSA must never contain:

- events
- observations
- decisions
- beliefs
- statistics
- reasoning artifacts

If something happened, it is not semantic.
If something was decided, it is not semantic.
If something might be true, it is not semantic.

Putting such content into KSA
corrupts the entire system.

---

## 5. Relationship to Cognition

Cognition may **consult** KSA.
It may not modify it.

Meaning flows one way:
from semantic authority into reasoning.

If reasoning is allowed to invent meaning,
the system begins to hallucinate structure.

---

## 6. Relationship to Institutional Memory

Institutional memory records what was claimed or decided.
KSA defines **what those claims mean**.

They are orthogonal:

- Memory without semantics is noise
- Semantics without memory is abstraction

Neither replaces the other.

---

## 7. Governance of Meaning

Changes to semantic authority are:

- rare
- explicit
- governed

Every semantic change:

- affects all past and future interpretation
- carries systemic risk

For this reason, meaning is treated
as one of the most sensitive assets of the system.

---

## 8. Typical Failure Modes

Common semantic failures include:

- encoding facts as definitions
- letting usage redefine meaning
- importing external ontologies uncritically
- allowing tools to mutate semantics
- treating popularity as correctness

Most epistemic collapses begin
as semantic collapses.

---

## 9. Practical Examples

Examples of semantic authority include:

- definition of a “claim”
- distinction between “signal” and “fact”
- meaning of “decision”
- scope of “validity”

These are not opinions.
They are constraints.

---

## Closing Note

Semantic authority is invisible when it works.

When it fails,
everything else fails silently.
