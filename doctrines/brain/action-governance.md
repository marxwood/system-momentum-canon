# Action / Governance Domain — Doctrinal View

This document explains the Action / Governance Domain
as the **only place where the system is allowed to act**.

It clarifies how decisions are made, why authority matters,
and where reasoning must end and responsibility must begin.

---

## 1. What Action and Governance Are

Action / Governance is the domain where the system
**crosses the line from thought to commitment**.

It is where:

- decisions are authorized
- responsibility is assumed
- consequences become real

Nothing becomes binding
until it passes through this domain.

---

## 2. Why Governance Is Necessary

Without governance:

- reasoning leaks into action
- tools begin to decide
- authority becomes implicit
- responsibility evaporates

Governance exists to ensure that:

- every action is attributable
- every decision is accountable
- no commitment is accidental

---

## 3. Boundary Between Reasoning and Action

The most critical boundary in the system
is the boundary between:

- _what could be done_
- and _what is done_

Cognition may propose.
Reflection may warn.
Only Action / Governance may decide.

Crossing this boundary prematurely
is the root of most systemic harm.

---

## 4. Inputs to Action / Governance

Governance decisions may consider:

- candidate actions from cognition
- semantic constraints from KSA
- historical context from IEM
- critique and signals from reflection

None of these are sufficient alone.

Action requires **explicit authorization**.

---

## 5. Outputs of Action

The outputs of this domain are:

- decisions
- commitments
- state changes

Every output:

- is recorded in institutional memory
- has provenance
- implies responsibility

If an action leaves no trace,
it was never governed.

---

## 6. What Governance Is Not

Governance is not:

- perception
- reasoning
- semantic definition
- reflection
- execution mechanics

It decides **that** something will happen,
not **how** it happens.

---

## 7. Authority and Legitimacy

Authority must be:

- explicit
- scoped
- auditable

Implicit authority
is indistinguishable from arbitrariness.

A system that cannot explain
_why_ it was allowed to act
has already failed.

---

## 8. Typical Failure Modes

Common governance failures include:

- bypassing governance for speed
- allowing tools to decide
- retroactively justifying actions
- erasing accountability
- confusing execution with authority

Most abuses of power
begin as “temporary shortcuts.”

---

## 9. Practical Examples

Examples of governed actions include:

- publishing an official claim
- changing an institutional position
- committing a decision
- entering a binding state

If it cannot be defended later,
it should not be done now.

---

## Closing Note

Governance is where systems
stop being clever
and start being responsible.

Everything before this domain is preparation.
Everything after it is consequence.
