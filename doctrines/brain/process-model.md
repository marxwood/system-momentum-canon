# Process Model — Doctrinal View

This document explains how work and processes are understood within Brain
**without modeling them as objects**.

It clarifies why Process is a sequence of Intents and Governance decisions,
not a persistent structure.

---

## 1. The Core Insight

Process is not an object.

Process is a **sequence of Intents and Governance decisions through time**.

If you try to model Process as an entity:

- You recreate the Task problem
- You store work instead of responsibility
- You confuse orchestration with epistemology

Brain does not see processes.
Brain sees consequences.

---

## 2. What a Process Actually Is

A process in Brain terms consists of:

1. **Intent declarations** — what the institution is trying to do
2. **Governance decisions** — who authorized what, under what conditions
3. **Domain flows** — which capabilities were activated
4. **IEM records** — what commitments resulted

The process itself is **not stored**.
Only its epistemic outputs are stored.

---

## 3. Example: Hotels Mandate Acquisition

Consider a real institutional process:

**Phase 1 — Exploration (no memory)**

- Intent: `exploratory`
- Cognitive analysis: "How do we get more mandates?"
- Result: Nothing is remembered

**Phase 2 — Strategic insight (Knowledge update)**

- Reflective input: "Mandates are the bottleneck"
- Knowledge update: Operational assumption recorded
- Result: Knowledge layer updated (not IEM)

**Phase 3 — Operational action (ephemeral)**

- Intent: `action`
- Governance decision: "Increase signal ingestion"
- Task created internally (ephemeral, not stored)
- Result: Work happens, Task is not remembered

**Phase 4 — Commitment (IEM record)**

- Intent: `epistemic_commitment`
- IEM record: Specific mandate recorded with authority and validity
- Result: This is the **only thing that persists**

---

## 4. What Gets Remembered

From the entire process above:

| What happened | Remembered? |
|---------------|-------------|
| Exploration thoughts | No |
| Strategic reasoning | No (unless formalized in Knowledge) |
| Task creation | No |
| Work performed | No |
| Mandate commitment | **Yes** (IEM) |

The institution remembers what it committed to.
It does not remember how it got there.

---

## 5. How "Where Did We Stop" Works

A common question: "If we don't remember processes, how do we know where we stopped?"

Answer: **We don't remember where we stopped. We reconstruct it.**

Reconstruction sources:

1. **IEM records** — what was decided, claimed, committed
2. **Knowledge state** — current operational understanding
3. **External world state** — what actually happened
4. **Governance state** — active policies, permissions, constraints

From these four sources, the system can always determine:

> "Given what we know and what we've committed to, what should we do next?"

The next step is **always re-decided**, never retrieved.

---

## 6. Why This Is Superior

Traditional systems remember:

- TODO lists
- Workflow state
- Progress markers

And therefore:

- They become stale
- They do stupid things because "that's what's written"
- They don't adapt to changed reality

Brain says:

> If the world changed, the next step must be recalculated.

This is deliberate rejection of false certainty.

---

## 7. Goals Are Not Objects

Goals may be:

- Temporal ("in the next 3 months")
- Epistemic ("increase claim reliability")
- Operational ("reduce cycle time")
- Strategic ("position as X")

But goals are **not memory objects**.

Goals:

- Influence Intent selection
- Influence Governance decisions
- Do not enter IEM (unless explicitly committed as institutional obligation)

A goal is a direction, not a record.

---

## 8. The Calendar Problem

Humans use calendars for orientation.

Calendars are:

- Not Brain memory
- Not institutional truth
- Not epistemic commitment

Calendars are **human scaffolding**.

Brain may receive calendar signals as percepts.
Brain does not treat calendars as authoritative.

You have a calendar.
Brain has responsibility.

This is a healthy separation.

---

## 9. How Humans Work With This Model

Practical coexistence:

| Human Tool | Brain Relationship |
|------------|-------------------|
| Calendar | Input signal (Perceptual) |
| TODO list | External orchestration (not Brain) |
| Project tracker | Coordination surface (Projection) |
| Meeting notes | Potential percepts or archival |

Humans may use any tools they want.
Brain does not import their structure.

When humans want something remembered institutionally,
they must create an explicit commitment through Governance.

---

## 10. Anti-Patterns

Common mistakes:

1. **Storing process state in IEM**
   Process is not truth. IEM is for truth.

2. **Treating Task completion as commitment**
   Completing a task is operational. Commitment requires explicit Intent.

3. **Assuming continuity from workflow**
   Workflows are external. Brain re-evaluates each time.

4. **Modeling goals as persistent entities**
   Goals direct; they don't persist unless committed.

---

## Closing Note

Brain does not remember the path.
Brain remembers the points where the institution said: "This is true."

Everything else is work.
And work, by design, disappears.

This is not forgetfulness.
This is institutional clarity.
