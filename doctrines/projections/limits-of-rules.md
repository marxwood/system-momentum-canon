# The Limits of Rules

Rules define boundaries.
They do not produce judgment.

This distinction is easy to forget
and costly to ignore.

---

## Why Rules Are Necessary

Rules are essential.

They establish:

- constraints
- prohibitions
- escalation paths
- authority boundaries

Without rules,
systems dissolve into arbitrariness.

The Canon exists precisely
to prevent that.

---

## Why Rules Are Insufficient

Rules cannot:

- anticipate every situation
- detect misuse in real time
- prevent motivated misinterpretation
- substitute for judgment

Every rule assumes
a reader acting in good faith
within an understood context.

When context shifts,
rules lag behind reality.

---

## Projection Misuse Is a Judgment Failure

Most projection failures
do not violate rules explicitly.

They exploit ambiguity.
They lean on urgency.
They hide behind interpretation.

From the inside,
everything appears compliant.

From the outside,
damage accumulates quietly.

No additional rule
would have prevented it.

---

## The Comfort of Formal Correctness

Rule compliance offers comfort.

If a decision follows procedure,
responsibility feels diluted.
If a projection follows format,
its effects feel justified.

This creates a dangerous equilibrium:
technically correct,
systemically wrong.

---

## Why More Rules Make Things Worse

Adding rules to address misuse
often increases complexity
without increasing clarity.

Complexity creates cover.
Cover enables selective reading.
Selective reading enables abuse.

At some point,
the rule system becomes
a projection of responsibility,
not a constraint on it.

---

## What Rules Cannot Enforce

Rules cannot enforce:

- epistemic humility
- tolerance for ambiguity
- willingness to surface bad news
- resistance to optics pressure
- discipline under urgency

These are cultural properties.
They emerge from practice,
not documentation.

---

## The Role of Doctrine

Doctrine exists
because rules cannot teach judgment.

Doctrine:

- names failure modes
- makes risks visible
- transmits hard-earned lessons
- prepares humans for situations
  where rules fall silent

Doctrine does not override rules.
It prepares people and agents
to act responsibly within them.

---

## Closing

Rules are the skeleton of a system.
They provide structure,
but no movement.

Projection discipline lives
in the space rules cannot reach.

Ignoring that space
is how formally correct systems fail.
