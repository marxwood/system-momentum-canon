# Projections — Doctrinal Overview

This document explains Projections from a human and operational perspective.

Projections are **ways of seeing** the system.
They are not ways of deciding, remembering, or defining truth.

Confusing projections with authority
is one of the most common and dangerous system errors.

---

## 1. What Projections Are

A projection is a **rendered view** of Brain state.

It exists to:

- make the system legible
- enable coordination
- support analysis
- communicate externally

A projection never changes what _is_.
It only changes what is _seen_.

---

## 2. What Projections Are Not

Projections are not:

- sources of truth
- commitments
- decisions
- memory
- governance mechanisms

If a projection disappears,
nothing canonical is lost.

If a projection is wrong,
the system is not corrupted —
only mis-seen.

---

## 3. Why Projections Exist at All

Without projections:

- systems become opaque
- coordination collapses
- humans cannot participate
- oversight becomes impossible

With too much trust in projections:

- appearances replace reality
- dashboards replace judgment
- metrics replace responsibility

Projections are a necessity,
not an authority.

---

## 4. Multiplicity of Projections

There is no single “correct” projection.

Different projections exist for:

- different audiences
- different intents
- different time horizons

Disagreement between projections
does not imply error.
It often reveals **different cuts of the same state**.

---

## 5. Projection Drift

A projection drifts when:

- it becomes optimized for appearance
- it is treated as performance
- it is taken as evidence of truth

When systems begin to govern
based on projections alone,
they stop governing reality.

---

## 6. Relationship to Canon and Brain

- Canon defines what exists and what matters
- Brain holds the authoritative state
- Projections expose partial views

Authority flows **downward**:
Canon → Brain → Projections

Never upward.

---

## 7. Typical Failure Modes

Common projection failures include:

- managing dashboards instead of reality
- acting to improve projections
- hiding problems by altering views
- mistaking visibility for control
- optimizing optics over substance

Many institutional failures
look good in projection until they collapse.

---

## 8. Practical Examples

Examples of projections include:

- public reports
- operational dashboards
- analytical summaries
- timelines
- filtered views

None of these are commitments.
They are **lenses**.

---

## Closing Note

Projections are how systems are seen.

They are not how systems decide,
remember, or justify themselves.

A system that governs projections
instead of reality
eventually governs nothing.
