# Delegation and Safety

Delegation is the act of granting consequence power.

The agent can only act within the boundaries the actor defines.

If boundaries are vague, delegation becomes unsafe.

---

## Delegation must be tiered

Not all tasks are equal.

Some tasks have low consequence radius.

Some tasks can cause:

- financial loss
- reputation damage
- relationship collapse
- or legal exposure

Delegation must be tiered by risk.

---

## What must not be delegated

The actor should not delegate:

- irreversible commitments
- threats or confrontational messages
- anything involving humiliation
- actions that create long-term dependence
- actions that require moral accountability

These are high-risk surfaces.

---

## Delegation must include refusal

Delegation must include refusal protocols.

The agent must be allowed to say:

- “insufficient context”
- “risk too high”
- “requires human decision”
- “unclear intent”

A delegator that forbids refusal creates unsafe autonomy.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is delegation-by-convenience.

Convenience delegation occurs when:

- the actor delegates because they are tired
- delegations become habitual
- and review disappears

Then failure becomes inevitable.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. delegation tiering by consequence radius
2. explicit “must not delegate” class
3. refusal protocols as safety mechanism
4. review before irreversible actions

If delegation is convenience-driven, autonomy becomes hazard.
