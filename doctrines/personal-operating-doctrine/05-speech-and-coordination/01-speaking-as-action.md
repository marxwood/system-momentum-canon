# Speaking as Action

Speaking is not neutral.

A statement is an act.

It changes what others can reasonably expect.

It changes what you can later deny.

This module treats speech as commitment-adjacent behavior.

---

## Speech creates obligations

Speech creates obligations in several ways:

- promises
- threats
- accusations
- declarations of loyalty
- declarations of refusal

Even casual speech can become binding when repeated.

Repeated speech becomes reputation.

Reputation becomes constraint.

---

## Speech is often used as discharge

Many people speak to discharge arousal.

Discharge speech is:

- fast
- moralized
- and absolute

Discharge speech feels like truth.

It is often self-regulation.

Self-regulation is not coordination.

---

## Speech must be slowed under arousal

Arousal compresses cognition.

Compressed cognition produces:

- certainty theater
- humiliation
- and escalation

The actor must treat arousal as a warning signal.

Not as clarity.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is speech-to-consequence denial.

Denial occurs when:

- the actor treats speech as “just words”
- and ignores the commitments created
- and refuses repair

Then trust collapses.

Coordination collapses.

---

## Failure modes

### Promise inflation

Saying yes to be liked.

### Threat drift

Using intimidation to feel safe.

### Accusation addiction

Blame becomes identity.

### Denial

Refusing responsibility for spoken harm.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. awareness that speech creates surfaces
2. slowed speech under arousal
3. refusal to use speech as discharge weapon
4. repair protocols for speech-caused harm

If speech is treated as free, the actor will create invisible coercion.
