# Intent Declaration

Intent is the internal permission layer.

If intent is not declared, action becomes:

- reactive
- narrative-driven
- and unaccountable

This module treats explicit intent as the first condition of personal legitimacy.

---

## Most intent is implicit

People often act without stating intent.

They later explain intent.

Later explanations are unreliable.

They are shaped by:

- shame avoidance
- status protection
- and narrative repair

This is not moral failure.

It is normal cognition.

It is also the root of drift.

---

## Declared intent prevents self-deception

When intent is declared:

- it becomes inspectable
- it becomes revisitable
- and it becomes correctable

The actor can later ask:

- did I do what I said I intended?
- did intent drift?
- did reality change?

Without declaration, none of these questions are answerable.

---

## Declared intent protects others

Intent declaration is not only personal.

It protects others from:

- hidden agendas
- emotional discharge framed as “truth”
- and unpredictable escalation

A person with declared intent is safer to coordinate with.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is post-hoc intent.

Post-hoc intent occurs when:

- action happens first
- and intent is invented afterward
- to preserve identity

Then the actor becomes unaccountable to themselves.

Self-accountability collapses.

---

## Failure modes

### Impulse rationalization

Desire becomes “reason”.

### Moral licensing

Intent is framed as virtue to excuse harm.

### Hidden revenge

Retaliation is reframed as justice.

### Avoidance intent

Avoidance is reframed as “priorities”.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. explicit intent before irreversible action
2. written intent for high-impact commitments
3. revision rights for intent as reality changes
4. post-action comparison: intent vs outcome

If intent is invented after action, drift becomes inevitable.
