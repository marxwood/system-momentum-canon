# Evidence Primitives

Personal life runs on claims.

Claims require evidence.

But personal evidence is not scientific evidence.

It is bounded, partial, and often ambiguous.

This module defines evidence primitives that preserve correction without pretending certainty.

---

## Evidence must be operational

Evidence is not “what feels true”.

Evidence is what can constrain interpretation.

A good evidence primitive is:

- observable
- shareable
- and revisitable

It reduces narrative freedom.

Narrative freedom is a danger under pressure.

---

## Evidence is often missing

Personal life often lacks strong evidence.

This is normal.

The doctrine does not attempt to eliminate ambiguity.

It attempts to prevent ambiguity from becoming certainty theater.

Certainty theater is worse than uncertainty.

---

## Examples of personal evidence primitives

Evidence primitives include:

- direct observation (“I heard X said”)
- written records (messages, notes)
- repeated patterns across time
- third-party corroboration
- material constraints (money, time, location)

They do not include:

- imagined motives
- identity-based assumptions
- “it must mean”
- emotional certainty

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is feeling-to-proof conversion.

Conversion occurs when:

- emotion is treated as evidence
- arousal is treated as clarity
- and narrative is treated as certainty

Then the actor becomes manipulable.

The actor becomes self-deceiving.

---

## Failure modes

### Motive hallucination

Motives replace evidence.

### Pattern overfitting

One event becomes destiny.

### Selective evidence

Only supportive signals are seen.

### Authority borrowing

Borrowing certainty from others to avoid ambiguity.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. a small set of revisitable evidence anchors
2. explicit rejection of emotion as proof
3. willingness to say “unknown”
4. separation of pattern from certainty

If evidence collapses, interpretation becomes tyranny.
