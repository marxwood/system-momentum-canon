# Observation vs Interpretation

The actor experiences the world through perception.

Perception immediately generates interpretation.

Interpretation is not optional.

But interpretation is not observation.

This module treats separation as the first epistemic skill.

---

## Interpretation is fast and sticky

Interpretation forms quickly.

It often becomes:

- identity
- certainty
- and permission to act

Once interpretation becomes permission, correction becomes humiliation.

Humiliation triggers defense.

Defense prevents revision.

---

## Observation is not neutrality

Observation is not emotionless.

Observation is:

- describing what happened
- without converting it into a story of what it means

Meaning can be assigned later.

Meaning assigned too early becomes trap.

---

## Interpretation is often a self-defense system

Many interpretations are not about truth.

They are about:

- protecting dignity
- protecting identity
- or avoiding shame

This is not moral failure.

It is normal cognition.

But it is dangerous cognition.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is interpretation-to-reality conversion.

Conversion occurs when:

- the actor treats meaning as fact
- and treats contradiction as attack
- and treats revision as humiliation

Then the actor becomes uncorrectable.

---

## Failure modes

### Mind-reading

Assuming motives as facts.

### Story certainty

A single explanation becomes law.

### Identity anchoring

Interpretation becomes “who I am”.

### Moral licensing

Interpretation grants permission to harm.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. explicit labeling of interpretation as interpretation
2. delayed meaning assignment under arousal
3. revision rights without humiliation
4. refusal to treat story as proof

If interpretation becomes reality, coordination collapses.
