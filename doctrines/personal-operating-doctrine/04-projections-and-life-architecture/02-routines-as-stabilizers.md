# Routines as Stabilizers

Routines are not discipline theater.

Routines are stabilizers.

They reduce decision load.

They preserve continuity.

This module treats routines as mechanisms that prevent drift.

---

## Routines prevent constant renegotiation

Without routines, the actor renegotiates life daily.

Daily renegotiation produces:

- fatigue
- inconsistency
- and shame loops

Shame loops destroy correction.

Routines prevent this by:

- fixing small defaults
- so the actor can spend agency on real decisions.

---

## Routines are not identity

Routines become dangerous when they become identity.

Identity routines produce:

- rigidity
- shame
- and moral superiority

A stabilizer that cannot be revised becomes coercion.

---

## Routines must be bounded

A routine should be:

- small
- stable
- and revisable

The goal is not maximal routine.

The goal is minimal stabilizers that preserve correction.

Over-routinization becomes prison.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is routine-to-morality conversion.

Conversion occurs when:

- routines become virtue
- deviation becomes shame
- and revision becomes humiliation

Then the actor becomes enslaved by stabilizers.

---

## Failure modes

### Routine absolutism

The routine is defended against reality.

### Shame deviation

Deviation triggers self-punishment.

### Expansion creep

Routines grow until life is consumed.

### Ritual replacement

Routines replace real intent.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. routines as stabilizers, not identity
2. bounded routines with explicit revision rights
3. refusal of shame-based discipline
4. periodic pruning of routines that became coercive

If routines become morality, they will become prison.
