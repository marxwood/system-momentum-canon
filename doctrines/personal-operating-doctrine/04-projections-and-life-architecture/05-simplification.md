# Simplification

Complexity is not depth.

Complexity is load.

Load creates fatigue.

Fatigue creates drift.

This module treats simplification as maintenance, not as minimalism ideology.

---

## Complexity produces hidden coercion

A complex life produces:

- too many commitments
- too many surfaces
- too many projections

The actor becomes governed by:

- reminders
- obligations
- and maintenance tasks

This is not freedom.

It is bureaucratic self-capture.

---

## Simplification is repair

Simplification is not aesthetics.

Simplification is:

- pruning commitments
- reducing surfaces
- removing redundant projections
- and restoring revision capacity

A system that cannot be simplified cannot be corrected.

---

## Simplification requires refusal

Most complexity persists because refusal is avoided.

Avoided refusal becomes:

- silent resentment
- avoidance
- and eventual collapse

Simplification is therefore a refusal discipline.

Refusal is part of repair.

---

## The personal hazard

The hazard is complexity-to-paralysis conversion.

Conversion occurs when:

- maintenance consumes agency
- revision becomes overwhelming
- and avoidance becomes normal

Then the actor cannot act.

The actor can only manage noise.

---

## Failure modes

### Commitment sprawl

Too many obligations.

### Projection sprawl

Too many lists, boards, dashboards.

### Maintenance obsession

The system consumes the life.

### Avoidance pruning

Simplification is postponed until crisis.

---

## Minimal implementation

A correction-capable actor preserves:

1. periodic pruning of commitments and projections
2. refusal protocols to prevent sprawl
3. simplification as repair, not as virtue
4. restoration of revision rights through reduction

If complexity cannot be reduced, the actor will drift into paralysis.
